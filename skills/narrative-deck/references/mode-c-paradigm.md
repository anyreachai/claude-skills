# Anyreach Narrative Deck Playbook — Mode C: The Paradigm Narrative

**The "Save Your Deck" Framework — Reverse-Engineered & Operationalized**

---

## Universal Principles

These apply to ALL three modes. They are non-negotiable structural rules derived from analyzing the Emergence Water, Elektra Health, and Fronteo decks.

### The One-Idea-Per-Slide Rule
Every slide conveys exactly ONE idea. If you can't summarize the slide's purpose in a single sentence, it needs to be split. The narrative storyboard (plain text, no design) must work on its own before any visual design begins.

### The Bold-Text Reading Path
Bold words within each slide form a readable sentence on their own. This is the "executive skimming layer" — a C-suite reader who only reads bold text should still get the full argument. When writing slide copy, write the bold words first, then fill in the rest.

### Progressive Commitment
The audience agrees to small, undeniable truths early in the deck. Each agreement makes it psychologically harder to reject bigger claims later. Structure the first 3-4 slides as a chain of increasingly specific truths that lead inevitably to your thesis.

### Storyboard-First, Design-Second
Lock the narrative in plain text before touching any design tool. The story must work in a text document. If it doesn't compel in plain text, no amount of design will save it.

### The Emotional Arc
Every deck follows a 4-act emotional structure:

| Act | Emotion | Slides | Purpose |
|-----|---------|--------|---------|
| Act 1: The World Is Broken | Shock / Outrage / Empathy | 1-4 | Make the audience feel the problem viscerally |
| Act 2: Nothing Works | Frustration / Hopelessness | 5-7 | Kill every alternative the audience might be thinking of |
| Act 3: We Figured It Out | Relief / Excitement | 8-12 | Introduce team, product, and proof |
| Act 4: Join Us | Urgency / FOMO | 13-15 | The ask, the scarcity, the vision |

### Mirror Structure
Every positive claim is set up by its negative mirror. "This is great / This is not great." "Too small / Big." "Hard to scale / Scalable." The contrast makes the positive land harder.

### Contrast Typography
Three tiers of text hierarchy on every slide:

- **Tier 1:** The "billboard" — 1-2 words or a number that can be read from across the room (42-60pt)
- **Tier 2:** The context — 1-2 sentences that explain what the billboard means (15-18pt)
- **Tier 3:** The evidence — supporting data, sources, footnotes (10-12pt)

## Mode C: The Paradigm Narrative

**Best for:** Series A investors, deep-tech audiences, industry analysts, anyone who needs to believe you're building a category, not just a product.

**Emotional fuel:** "The world works differently than you think, and we've figured out the pattern that everyone else is missing."

**Core thesis:** Every generation of customer experience technology followed the same pattern — first it automated the channel, then it automated the agent's workflow, then it automated the agent itself. We're in the third shift, and the companies that see it first will define the next decade.

## Mode C — Discovery Conversation

### Section 1: The Historical Pattern (Slides 1-5)

**Q1: The Origin Analogy**
We need a story like the Lovastatin/chicken-feed anecdote — an unexpected connection that led to a breakthrough. Possible angles:
- The origin of the contact center itself (what cross-industry insight created it?)
- An analogy from a completely different field that mirrors what's happening in CX now
- A specific moment in tech history where two unrelated technologies collided and created a new category

Tell me a story — from any field — about an unexpected insight that changed everything. Ideally something your audience wouldn't expect.

**Q2: The Pattern of Breakthroughs**
The Fronteo deck listed: Microwave, X-Ray, Velcro, CRISPR, Gravity — all examples of cross-domain observations creating breakthroughs. What's our list? This should show the pattern in customer experience technology specifically:
- 1960s: ACD (Automatic Call Distributor) — telephony meets queuing theory
- 1990s: IVR — voice recognition meets call routing
- 2000s: Cloud contact center (Five9, etc.) — internet meets telephony
- 2010s: Chatbots — NLP meets customer service
- 2020s: Agentic AI — LLMs meet resolution orchestration

List 4-5 generational leaps in CX technology, each framed as "X meets Y = breakthrough."

**Q3: The Missing Capability**
The Fronteo deck named "abductive reasoning" as the thing LLMs can't do. What's the named capability that current contact center AI lacks?
- "Contextual resolution" — understanding the full context and actually solving the problem
- "Agentic persistence" — staying on the task until it's done
- "Cross-channel memory" — knowing what happened on chat when the customer calls
- "Intent orchestration" — understanding what the customer actually needs, not just what they said

What's the specific, nameable capability that separates real AI agents from everything else? It needs to be real enough to be defensible and distinctive enough to own.

**Q4: Why Current AI Can't Do It**
The Fronteo deck called out OpenAI, Anthropic, etc. as lacking abductive reasoning. What's our version?
- "Basic chatbots can answer questions. They can't resolve problems."
- "LLMs can generate text. They can't orchestrate actions across systems."
- "Current AI can handle a single turn of conversation. It can't manage a multi-step, multi-channel resolution."

What's the structural limitation of current AI in the contact center space? Be specific about the architectural gap.

### Section 2: Product & Proof (Slides 6-10)

**Q5: "Meet [Product Name]"**
After building the intellectual scaffolding, the product reveal should feel like a revelation. How do you want to introduce it?
- "Meet Anyreach. The first agentic AI platform built for [the named capability]."
- "Meet Anyreach. [Capability name], deployed."

What's the one-line introduction that ties the product directly to the paradigm?

**Q6: Case Studies**
We need 2-3 case studies following the format: Context → What Anyreach did → Result. Each should prove a different aspect of the paradigm:
- Case Study 1: Proves speed/scale (like Fronteo's "2 days, 6 novel targets")
- Case Study 2: Proves predictive power or cross-domain applicability
- Case Study 3: Proves the business impact (revenue, cost savings, NPS)

For each case study:
- Client name (or anonymized descriptor)
- The problem they faced
- What Anyreach specifically did (be concrete)
- The quantified result (resolution rate, cost savings, time to deploy, etc.)
- Why this result was surprising or disproportionate

Give me 2-3 case studies with specific numbers.

### Section 3: Company & Vision (Slides 11-14)

**Q7: The Company Snapshot**
Fronteo showed: 388 clients, 350M+ sentences analyzed, 20+ years in AI, 206 employees, 10+ pharma partnerships, 91 patents. What are our equivalent proof points?
- Number of client companies
- Volume of interactions processed
- Years building AI / in the CX space
- Team size
- Patents or proprietary technology
- Annual revenue

Give me 5-7 quantified company proof points.

**Q8: Logo Wall / Trust Signals**
Who are the notable clients, partners, or investors we can display?
- Enterprise client logos (with permission)
- BPO partner logos
- Investor logos
- Technology partner logos
- Certification/compliance badges

List all logos we can display, categorized by type.

**Q9: The Platform Vision**
The Fronteo close was "Be the starting point for every new drug. We find the targets. Partners build the treatments." What's our equivalent?
- "Be the resolution layer for every customer interaction."
- "We resolve the conversations. Partners own the relationships."
- "Every enterprise will have an AI workforce. We build that workforce."

What's the 2-sentence vision that positions Anyreach as infrastructure, not just a product?

**Q10-Q12: Same as Mode A Q14-Q16** (Scarcity, the ask, vision close)

## Appendix: Visual Design System

### Anyreach Brand System (Quick Reference for Deck Generation)

All decks should follow the Anyreach design system defined in `/mnt/skills/user/anyreach-startek-deck/references/design-system.md`. Key specs:

**Layout:** 16:9 (10" × 5.625"), margins 0.6" left/right, 0.5" top/bottom

**Non-Negotiable Elements:**
- Pink top bar (#EC4899) on EVERY slide — 0.06" height, full width
- Cards over bullets — never use raw bullet lists
- Icons in colored circles — never naked icons
- Accent color cycling: indigo → cyan → green → amber → pink
- Footer: "CONFIDENTIAL" left, "anyreach.ai" right, 10pt muted

**Backgrounds:**
- Dark slides: #1A1A2E (title, section dividers, ask, close)
- Light slides: #F5F6FA (all content slides)
- Cards on light bg: #252550 with colored top border
- Cards on light bg (alt): White with colored left border

**Typography:**
- Font: Calibri (always)
- Section labels: 12pt Bold, ALL CAPS, accent colored
- Slide titles: 42pt Bold
- Body: 12-15pt Regular
- Stats: 42-60pt Bold, accent colored
- Captions/footer: 10pt muted

**Color Roles:**
- Green (#10B981) = positive, good, growth, CTA
- Coral (#F87171) = negative, pain, "before"
- Indigo (#5B5FC7) = primary accent, first in cycle
- Cyan (#06B6D4) = secondary accent
- Amber (#F59E0B) = tertiary accent, timelines
- Pink (#EC4899) = brand bar, section labels
