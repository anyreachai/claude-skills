# Anyreach Narrative Deck Playbook — Mode B: The Empathy Narrative

**The "Save Your Deck" Framework — Reverse-Engineered & Operationalized**

---

## Universal Principles

These apply to ALL three modes. They are non-negotiable structural rules derived from analyzing the Emergence Water, Elektra Health, and Fronteo decks.

### The One-Idea-Per-Slide Rule
Every slide conveys exactly ONE idea. If you can't summarize the slide's purpose in a single sentence, it needs to be split. The narrative storyboard (plain text, no design) must work on its own before any visual design begins.

### The Bold-Text Reading Path
Bold words within each slide form a readable sentence on their own. This is the "executive skimming layer" — a C-suite reader who only reads bold text should still get the full argument. When writing slide copy, write the bold words first, then fill in the rest.

### Progressive Commitment
The audience agrees to small, undeniable truths early in the deck. Each agreement makes it psychologically harder to reject bigger claims later. Structure the first 3-4 slides as a chain of increasingly specific truths that lead inevitably to your thesis.

### Storyboard-First, Design-Second
Lock the narrative in plain text before touching any design tool. The story must work in a text document. If it doesn't compel in plain text, no amount of design will save it.

### The Emotional Arc
Every deck follows a 4-act emotional structure:

| Act | Emotion | Slides | Purpose |
|-----|---------|--------|---------|
| Act 1: The World Is Broken | Shock / Outrage / Empathy | 1-4 | Make the audience feel the problem viscerally |
| Act 2: Nothing Works | Frustration / Hopelessness | 5-7 | Kill every alternative the audience might be thinking of |
| Act 3: We Figured It Out | Relief / Excitement | 8-12 | Introduce team, product, and proof |
| Act 4: Join Us | Urgency / FOMO | 13-15 | The ask, the scarcity, the vision |

### Mirror Structure
Every positive claim is set up by its negative mirror. "This is great / This is not great." "Too small / Big." "Hard to scale / Scalable." The contrast makes the positive land harder.

### Contrast Typography
Three tiers of text hierarchy on every slide:

- **Tier 1:** The "billboard" — 1-2 words or a number that can be read from across the room (42-60pt)
- **Tier 2:** The context — 1-2 sentences that explain what the billboard means (15-18pt)
- **Tier 3:** The evidence — supporting data, sources, footnotes (10-12pt)

## Mode B: The Empathy Narrative

**Best for:** Enterprise buyers who interact with customers (NWFCU, Centene, Fabletics), healthcare/financial services where the end-customer experience matters deeply, anyone whose customers are suffering.

**Emotional fuel:** "Your customers are suffering, your agents are suffering, and the current system was never designed to help either of them."

**Core thesis:** The people calling your contact center are often at their most frustrated, confused, or vulnerable — and they're met with hold music, scripted responses, and transfers to nowhere. This isn't just bad customer experience — it's a failure of care.

---

## Mode B — Discovery Conversation

### Section 1: The Human Pain (Slides 1-4)

**Q1: The Blunt Opener**
What is the most honest, unvarnished thing a customer would say about the experience of calling a contact center? We need a "Going through menopause sucks" equivalent — something so candid it cuts through corporate language.
- "Calling customer service sucks."
- "Nobody actually wants to call your company."
- "Your customers hate calling you. Your agents hate answering."
- Something more specific to the vertical?

What's the raw, honest version of what customers feel? Don't sanitize it.

**Q2: The Absurd Gap**
What's the equivalent of "0.003% chance of seeing a specialist"? A stat that shows how broken the experience actually is for the person on the other end of the phone.
- Percentage of calls that resolve on first contact
- Average hold time + transfer count for a "simple" issue
- Percentage of customers who give up and churn rather than call back
- Cost to the customer in time/frustration for something that should take 2 minutes

Give me the number that makes the audience think "how is this even possible in 2026?"

**Q3: The Emotional Laundry List**
What does a bad contact center experience lead to for the customer? The Elektra deck listed "depression, weight gain, brain fog, heart disease, dementia, osteoporosis, musculoskeletal pain, incontinence." What's our list?
- Lost time
- Financial consequences of unresolved issues
- Emotional frustration that erodes brand loyalty
- Specific consequences by vertical (healthcare: delayed care; financial services: missed payments; retail: abandoned purchases)

What are the downstream consequences of a single bad interaction, especially in your key verticals?

**Q4: The Scale of Suffering**
How many people experience this? We need the "80M women over 40" equivalent.
- Total annual contact center interactions in the US
- Number of customers who experience the specific pain you described
- Segment-specific numbers (e.g., "X million credit union members calling about the same 10 issues")

How many people are affected, and how often?

### Section 2: The Failed Journey (Slides 5-8)

**Q5: The Customer's Actual Journey**
Walk me through what a real customer experiences today, step by step. The Elektra deck used the patient journey (go to PCP → get told to tough it out → get prescriptions yourself → nothing works). What's our version?
- Step 1: Customer has a problem → [what they do first]
- Step 2: They try self-service → [what happens]
- Step 3: They call → [what happens]
- Step 4: They get transferred → [what happens]
- Step 5: They give up → [what happens to them / to the enterprise]

Walk me through the worst-case (but common) customer journey in one of your key verticals.

**Q6: "None of These Address the Root Problem"**
After walking through the journey, what's the fundamental thing that's broken?
- "The system is designed to deflect, not resolve."
- "Every touchpoint assumes the customer will figure it out themselves."
- "The root problem is that no one owns the customer's outcome."

What is the root failure that every step in the journey shares?

**Q7: "It's Not Just Bad for Customers — It's Bad for [Buyer]"**
This is the pivot from empathy to business case. The Elektra deck pivoted from "bad for women" to "bad for insurers — $15K per patient, $1B+ total." What's our equivalent?
- Cost of agent turnover (recruiting, training, lost productivity)
- Revenue lost to churn caused by bad service
- Operational cost of repeat calls, escalations, supervisor time
- Compliance/regulatory risk of poorly handled interactions

Give me the dollar amounts that prove bad CX isn't just a feelings problem — it's a P&L problem.

### Section 3: Founder & Product (Slides 9-12)

**Q8: The "What Should Have Existed" Story**
The Elektra deck positioned the product as "creating the coordinated, wholistic care women deserve that should have existed for the past 100 years." What's our version?
- "We built the intelligent, always-available agent that should have existed the moment AI became capable enough."
- "We created what every customer assumes already exists when they call — someone who actually knows them, understands them, and can help them."

How do you describe Anyreach as correcting an injustice, not just filling a market gap?

**Q9-Q13: Same as Mode A Q8-Q12** (Founder story, team, traction, product, disproportionate result)

### Section 4: Business Model & Ask (Slides 13-16)

**Q14: The Business Model as Moat**
The Elektra deck's "The best part? Insurers pay for it" was both a business model explanation and a moat. What's our equivalent?
- "Enterprises pay per resolution, not per seat — aligned incentives."
- "Our BPO partners distribute us to their entire client base."
- Something about the data flywheel or multi-channel lock-in?

What's the sentence that simultaneously explains how you make money AND why it's hard to replicate?

**Q15-Q16: Same as Mode A Q14-Q16** (Scarcity, the ask, vision close)

## Mode B — Deck Generation Spec

### Slide 1: Title Slide

Same structure as Mode A Slide 1.

---

### Slide 2: The Blunt Opener

**Narrative storyboard:**
> [Blunt, colloquial statement in huge text]
> [Subtext with emotional specifics]

**Example:**
> **Calling customer service sucks.**
> Especially when you're [specific situation: trying to dispute a charge / asking about your prescription / figuring out your mortgage].

**Visual design:**
- Dark background (#1A1A2E) — this slide needs weight
- Pink top bar
- Giant text: The blunt statement in 42pt Bold, ALL CAPS, white
- Below: The context in 18pt, muted (#B8BCD0)
- No cards, no icons, no stats — pure emotional impact
- Optional: A muted image background (contact center stock photo at 10-15% opacity) to add texture

**Design notes:** This slide is the equivalent of Elektra's "GOING THROUGH MENOPAUSE SUCKS." It should feel like a billboard, not a slide.

---

### Slide 3: The Absurd Gap

**Narrative storyboard:**
> And today,
> [the likelihood / the percentage / the chance] of [resolution] is [absurdly low number].
> [Supporting stats: total interactions, percentage that fail, etc.]

**Visual design:**
- Light background (#F5F6FA)
- Section label: "AND TODAY" in ALL CAPS, coral (#F87171)
- Large text: The key stat sentence in 36pt Bold, dark
- Below: 2-3 supporting stat boxes in a row (dark cards with colored top borders)
- The absurd number itself should be pulled out in 60pt Bold, coral

---

### Slide 4: The Scale + Downstream Consequences

**Narrative storyboard:**
> [Total number of affected interactions/people]
> It comes with a wave of consequences:
> [List of downstream effects, bolded for emphasis]

**Visual design:**
- Light background (#F5F6FA)
- Left side: Large number (total affected) in 60pt Bold, dark, with label below
- Right side: List of consequences as compact dark cards stacked vertically, each with a coral left border and bold text
- No happy colors on this slide — coral and dark tones only

---

### Slide 5: Customer Journey Step 1

**Narrative storyboard:**
> This leaves [X] options for customers.
> **01**: [First thing they try]
> [Why it fails]
> [Consequence]

**Visual design:**
- Light background (#F5F6FA)
- Section label: "THE CUSTOMER JOURNEY" in ALL CAPS, amber
- Large "01" in 60pt, muted, bottom-left
- Left panel: "This leaves two options for customers." in 36pt Bold
- Right panel: The first option described in a white card with amber left border
  - What the customer does
  - What happens (the failure)
  - The quote that captures how it feels: "What you are experiencing is normal. Figure it out."

### Slide 6: Customer Journey Step 2

Same structure as Slide 5 with "02" — the second failed option.

### Slide 7: "None of These Address the Root Problem"

**Visual design:**

### Slide 8: The Business Case Pivot

**Narrative storyboard:**
> And that problem isn't just bad for customers.
> It is bad for [the buyer / enterprises / insurers].
> [Dollar amounts proving the P&L impact]

**Visual design:**
- Light background (#F5F6FA)
- Section label: "THE COST" in ALL CAPS, coral
- Title: "And that problem isn't just bad for customers." in 36pt Bold
- Subtitle: "It is bad for [buyer persona]." in 24pt Bold, coral
- Two large stat cards:
  - $[X] per [unit of failure] (dark card, coral top border)
  - $[Y] total addressable waste (dark card, coral top border)
- Bottom callout bar: Punchline connecting customer pain to business loss, green text

The founder, team, traction, product, comparison, benefits, scarcity, ask, and vision close follow the same structure. The only difference is that the product reveal (Slide 12) should frame each feature as "what the customer deserves" rather than "what replaces the failed alternative."

## Appendix: Visual Design System

### Anyreach Brand System (Quick Reference for Deck Generation)

All decks should follow the Anyreach design system defined in `/mnt/skills/user/anyreach-startek-deck/references/design-system.md`. Key specs:

**Layout:** 16:9 (10" × 5.625"), margins 0.6" left/right, 0.5" top/bottom

**Non-Negotiable Elements:**
- Pink top bar (#EC4899) on EVERY slide — 0.06" height, full width
- Cards over bullets — never use raw bullet lists
- Icons in colored circles — never naked icons
- Accent color cycling: indigo → cyan → green → amber → pink
- Footer: "CONFIDENTIAL" left, "anyreach.ai" right, 10pt muted

**Backgrounds:**
- Dark slides: #1A1A2E (title, section dividers, ask, close)
- Light slides: #F5F6FA (all content slides)
- Cards on light bg: #252550 with colored top border
- Cards on light bg (alt): White with colored left border

**Typography:**
- Font: Calibri (always)
- Section labels: 12pt Bold, ALL CAPS, accent colored
- Slide titles: 42pt Bold
- Body: 12-15pt Regular
- Stats: 42-60pt Bold, accent colored
- Captions/footer: 10pt muted

**Color Roles:**
- Green (#10B981) = positive, good, growth, CTA
- Coral (#F87171) = negative, pain, "before"
- Indigo (#5B5FC7) = primary accent, first in cycle
- Cyan (#06B6D4) = secondary accent
- Amber (#F59E0B) = tertiary accent, timelines
- Pink (#EC4899) = brand bar, section labels
