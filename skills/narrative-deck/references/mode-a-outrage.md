# Anyreach Narrative Deck Playbook — Mode A: The Outrage Narrative

**The "Save Your Deck" Framework — Reverse-Engineered & Operationalized**

---

## Universal Principles

These apply to ALL three modes. They are non-negotiable structural rules derived from analyzing the Emergence Water, Elektra Health, and Fronteo decks.

### The One-Idea-Per-Slide Rule
Every slide conveys exactly ONE idea. If you can't summarize the slide's purpose in a single sentence, it needs to be split. The narrative storyboard (plain text, no design) must work on its own before any visual design begins.

### The Bold-Text Reading Path
Bold words within each slide form a readable sentence on their own. This is the "executive skimming layer" — a C-suite reader who only reads bold text should still get the full argument. When writing slide copy, write the bold words first, then fill in the rest.

### Progressive Commitment
The audience agrees to small, undeniable truths early in the deck. Each agreement makes it psychologically harder to reject bigger claims later. Structure the first 3-4 slides as a chain of increasingly specific truths that lead inevitably to your thesis.

### Storyboard-First, Design-Second
Lock the narrative in plain text before touching any design tool. The story must work in a text document. If it doesn't compel in plain text, no amount of design will save it.

### The Emotional Arc
Every deck follows a 4-act emotional structure:

| Act | Emotion | Slides | Purpose |
|-----|---------|--------|---------|
| Act 1: The World Is Broken | Shock / Outrage / Empathy | 1-4 | Make the audience feel the problem viscerally |
| Act 2: Nothing Works | Frustration / Hopelessness | 5-7 | Kill every alternative the audience might be thinking of |
| Act 3: We Figured It Out | Relief / Excitement | 8-12 | Introduce team, product, and proof |
| Act 4: Join Us | Urgency / FOMO | 13-15 | The ask, the scarcity, the vision |

### Mirror Structure
Every positive claim is set up by its negative mirror. "This is great / This is not great." "Too small / Big." "Hard to scale / Scalable." The contrast makes the positive land harder.

### Contrast Typography
Three tiers of text hierarchy on every slide:

- **Tier 1:** The "billboard" — 1-2 words or a number that can be read from across the room (42-60pt)
- **Tier 2:** The context — 1-2 sentences that explain what the billboard means (15-18pt)
- **Tier 3:** The evidence — supporting data, sources, footnotes (10-12pt)

## Mode A: The Outrage Narrative

**Best for:** BPO partners (Startek, CP360), enterprise prospects where the buyer is an operations/finance leader, anyone whose budget is being wasted by the current system.

**Emotional fuel:** "The current system is absurd, wasteful, and everyone knows it."

**Core thesis:** Contact centers are a $400B+ industry built on a model that fails customers, burns out agents, and hemorrhages money — and the "solutions" everyone is buying (basic chatbots, offshore labor, legacy IVR) make it worse.

## Mode A — Discovery Conversation

The following questions extract the raw material needed to populate every slide. Ask these in order — each builds on the previous answer.

### Section 1: The Problem (Slides 1-4)

**Q1: The Jaw-Dropping Stat**
What is the single most shocking statistic about the contact center industry that most people don't know? We need a number so absurd it reframes the audience's understanding. Examples of the kind of thing we're looking for:
- Total annual spend on contact centers globally or in the US
- Percentage of calls that don't resolve on first contact
- Average cost per call vs. average value per call
- Number of agent hours wasted on calls that could be automated
- Rate of call abandonment or customer callback within 48 hours

What's the stat AND what's the source so we can footnote it?

**Q2: The Acceleration / Trajectory**
Is the problem getting worse? We need a "and it's expected to double/triple" type data point. For example:
- Contact center costs increasing year-over-year
- Agent turnover rates climbing
- Customer expectations rising faster than service quality
- Volume of customer interactions growing while budgets stay flat

What's the trend line AND over what time horizon?

**Q3: The Moral Tension / Dual Perspective**
Who benefits from the current broken system, and who suffers? We need the "This is great if you're X / This is not great if you're Y" framing. Possible angles:
- "This is great if you're a legacy contact center vendor charging per-seat licenses / This is not great if you're an enterprise paying $15 per call that doesn't resolve"
- "This is great if you're a BPO charging per-agent-hour / This is not great if you're a customer waiting 23 minutes for a scripted response"

Who specifically profits from the inefficiency, and who suffers? Give me the companies/personas on each side.

**Q4: The Absurd Comparison**
We need a comparison that makes the inefficiency feel obscene. The Emergence deck used "1,313x water use vs. residents." The Elektra deck used "0.003% chance of seeing a specialist." What's our equivalent?
- Cost of one unresolved call chain (initial + callback + escalation + churn) vs. cost of AI resolution
- Number of agent hours spent on calls that could be fully automated vs. total agent hours
- Enterprise spend on contact center tech that goes unused or underperforms

Give me two numbers that, when placed side by side, make anyone say "that's insane."

### Section 2: Failed Alternatives (Slides 5-7)

**Q5: The Three Failed Paths**
What are the 2-3 things enterprises have already tried (or are currently buying) that don't work? For each, we need:
- What it is (e.g., "Offshore BPO," "Legacy IVR trees," "Basic chatbots")
- Why it seems like a good idea on paper
- The specific, structural reason it fails (not opinion — a mechanical/architectural limitation)
- A stat or data point that proves the failure

List 2-3 alternatives with their specific failure modes.

**Q6: The Kill Shot**
After listing the alternatives, what's the one-sentence summary of WHY none of them work? The Elektra equivalent was "Neither of these address the root problem." What's ours?
- "None of these actually resolve the customer's issue"
- "All of these optimize for cost per interaction, not outcome per interaction"
- Something else?

What's the root problem that every alternative misses?

### Section 3: The Shared Aspiration (Slide 8)

**Q7: The Unicorn Everyone Wants**
What do ALL stakeholders (enterprises, BPOs, customers, agents) actually want? The Emergence equivalent was "self-reliant data centers." What's the thing everyone is chasing?
- "Intelligent, always-on customer resolution"
- "Zero-wait, first-contact resolution at a fraction of current cost"
- Something more specific?

What's the aspiration that unites the buyer, the end-customer, and the agent?

### Section 4: Team & Prior Art (Slides 9-11)

**Q8: Founder Origin / Why You**
Tell me the story of why YOU specifically are the person to build this. We need:
- Your Five9 background — what did you see there that others didn't?
- The specific moment or insight that led to Anyreach
- Any "epiphany" or pivot moment (like Elektra's "despite that success, we were not solving the water crisis")
- Credentials that answer specific objections (technical depth, enterprise sales experience, domain expertise)

Give me the 2-minute version of your founder story, emphasizing what you saw that others missed.

**Q9: Team Credentials**
For each key team member (up to 6), I need:
- Name and title
- 1-sentence bio emphasizing the most relevant credential
- 1-2 quantified achievements (years experience, companies built, revenue driven, etc.)
- Which specific objection their presence on the team answers (e.g., "Can you actually build enterprise software?" → CTO's track record)

List your key team members with their strongest proof points.

**Q10: Prior Art / Existing Traction**
What have you already built and proven? We need:
- Current MRR and growth trajectory
- Number and names of enterprise customers (with permission to reference)
- Specific deployments that prove the technology works
- Any "despite that success" pivot story — did you start with something simpler and realize you needed to go bigger?

What's the proof that this isn't just a pitch — it's already working?

### Section 5: Product & Proof (Slides 12-14)

**Q11: The Product Reveal**
Describe the product in a way that directly mirrors and resolves the failed alternatives from Q5. For each failed alternative, show how Anyreach solves that specific failure:
- Failed: Offshore BPO → Anyreach: [how you replace/augment this]
- Failed: Legacy IVR → Anyreach: [how you replace this]
- Failed: Basic chatbot → Anyreach: [how you're fundamentally different]

For each failed path, what's the Anyreach equivalent that actually works?

**Q12: The Disproportionate Result**
We need a competitive comparison so lopsided it's almost absurd, like Emergence's "more water in 1 day than our closest competitor could in 1 year." What's ours?
- Resolution rate vs. industry average
- Cost per resolution vs. traditional model
- Time to deploy vs. legacy implementation
- Volume of interactions handled vs. headcount equivalent

Give me the comparison that makes the audience's jaw drop.

**Q13: The Benefits Grid**
List 4-6 benefits of the Anyreach platform, each in 2-4 words. These should be the "rational confirmation" layer — the checklist that lets the analytical audience justify the emotional decision they've already made. Examples:
- Omnichannel Resolution
- 90% Cost Reduction
- Deploy in Weeks
- No Agent Burnout
- Enterprise-Grade Compliance
- Scales to Millions

What are the 4-6 benefits that would appear on a features comparison grid?

### Section 6: The Ask (Slides 15-16)

**Q14: Scarcity / Urgency**
What creates urgency? The Elektra deck used "We can only serve 10% of current demand — we're leaving $1M ARR on the table." What's our version?
- Pipeline value vs. current capacity to deliver
- Deals in progress that need scaling to close
- Market window that's closing (competitors raising, buyers consolidating)

What's the "if you don't invest now, here's what we lose" argument?

**Q15: The Ask**
What specifically are you raising, and what will it fund? Structure as:
- Raising: $[X]
- To achieve: [2-3 specific milestones]
- Target outcome: $[Y] ARR by [date]

Give me the raise amount, the use of funds, and the target ARR.

**Q16: The Vision Close**
What's the aspirational one-liner that closes the deck? This should be about the mission, not the product. Examples:
- "Every customer interaction, resolved intelligently. Every enterprise, freed from the contact center tax."
- "The last generation automated the channel. We're automating the resolution."

What's the sentence you want ringing in the audience's ears as they leave?

## Appendix: Visual Design System

### Anyreach Brand System (Quick Reference for Deck Generation)

All decks should follow the Anyreach design system defined in `/mnt/skills/user/anyreach-startek-deck/references/design-system.md`. Key specs:

**Layout:** 16:9 (10" × 5.625"), margins 0.6" left/right, 0.5" top/bottom

**Non-Negotiable Elements:**
- Pink top bar (#EC4899) on EVERY slide — 0.06" height, full width
- Cards over bullets — never use raw bullet lists
- Icons in colored circles — never naked icons
- Accent color cycling: indigo → cyan → green → amber → pink
- Footer: "CONFIDENTIAL" left, "anyreach.ai" right, 10pt muted

**Backgrounds:**
- Dark slides: #1A1A2E (title, section dividers, ask, close)
- Light slides: #F5F6FA (all content slides)
- Cards on light bg: #252550 with colored top border
- Cards on light bg (alt): White with colored left border

**Typography:**
- Font: Calibri (always)
- Section labels: 12pt Bold, ALL CAPS, accent colored
- Slide titles: 42pt Bold
- Body: 12-15pt Regular
- Stats: 42-60pt Bold, accent colored
- Captions/footer: 10pt muted

**Color Roles:**
- Green (#10B981) = positive, good, growth, CTA
- Coral (#F87171) = negative, pain, "before"
- Indigo (#5B5FC7) = primary accent, first in cycle
- Cyan (#06B6D4) = secondary accent
- Amber (#F59E0B) = tertiary accent, timelines
- Pink (#EC4899) = brand bar, section labels
