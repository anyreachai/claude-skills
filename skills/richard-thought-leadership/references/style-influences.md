# Style Influences & Patterns

These are the writing styles and structural patterns Richard admires and draws from. Use these as references when crafting content.

---

## Ivan Zhao (Notion CEO) — "Steam, Steel, and Infinite Minds"

### What makes it work:
- **Triple historical metaphor as backbone.** Steel (buildings/organizations), steam (factories/workflows), Florence-to-megacities (economies). Each metaphor explores the same thesis at a different scale.
- **Personal stake woven in naturally.** "At my company Notion, we have been experimenting" — not a pitch, just evidence from his own experience.
- **Marshall McLuhan framing.** "Driving into the future via the rearview window" — borrows from a known thinker to elevate the argument.
- **Concrete specifics.** "More than 700 agents now handle repetitive work" — not "we use AI extensively." Numbers ground the vision.
- **Images as historical evidence.** Photos of Carnegie as a boy, Pittsburgh factories, the Woolworth Building, Red Flag Act illustration. Each image earns its place by adding context the text alone can't.
- **Closing callback.** Final line returns to all three metaphors: "Steel. Steam. Infinite minds. The next skyline is there, waiting for us to build it."

### Structural pattern:
1. Historical anchor (Carnegie, steel)
2. Personal connection ("I run a software company")
3. Metaphor applied at individual scale (bicycles → cars)
4. Metaphor applied at organizational scale (steel frames, steam engines)
5. Metaphor applied at economic scale (Florence → megacities)
6. Call to action / closing callback

## Cursor Team / Sequoia — "The Third Era of AI Software Development"

### What makes it work:
- **Metric-driven credibility.** "35% of PRs we merge are now created by agents" — specific, verifiable, impressive.
- **Era framing.** Tab → Agents → Cloud Agents. Simple progression that makes the reader feel the trajectory.
- **Product evolution as narrative.** The piece reads as a story of how their own product evolved, which naturally demonstrates the market shift.
- **Understated confidence.** "A year from now, we think the vast majority of development work will be done by these kinds of agents" — bold prediction delivered calmly.
- **Usage data as proof.** The 15x growth chart, the 2.5x flip from Tab to Agent users. Let the data make the argument.

### Structural pattern:
1. Where we started (Tab autocomplete)
2. What changed (agents arrived)
3. What's changing now (cloud agents)
4. How we're already working this way (internal data)
5. What's next (implicit prediction)

## Henry Ford Cautionary Tale Pattern

### What makes it work:
- **Single dramatic story as the entire argument.** One company, one failure, one lesson. No need for multiple examples.
- **Specific details create credibility.** "55% to barely 10%," "60,000 workers laid off," "15,000 machine tools replaced." The numbers tell the story.
- **The people who saw it coming.** Edsel, Kanzler, the engineers who built a secret prototype. This adds dramatic tension — it wasn't an unforeseeable disaster.
- **Universal lesson extracted.** "What got you here will kill you there." Simple enough to remember and repeat.
- **Modern parallels as coda.** Kodak, Nokia, Blockbuster — a quick cascade that says "this pattern is not unique to Ford."

### Structural pattern:
1. The milestone that was actually a funeral (15 millionth Model T)
2. The dominance before the fall (55% market share)
3. The market shift Ford ignored
4. The people who tried to warn him
5. The collapse and costly recovery
6. The universal principle
7. Modern echoes

## Common Patterns Across All Three

1. **Concrete before abstract.** Always start with a specific story, data point, or observation — never with the thesis statement.
2. **The author has skin in the game.** Each writer is building something in the space they're writing about. The authority comes from doing, not observing.
3. **Historical patterns as predictive tools.** History isn't decoration — it's the evidence for why the future will unfold a certain way.
4. **Frameworks that compress complexity.** "Bicycle → car → self-driving." "Waterwheel → steam → electricity." "Tab → agent → cloud agent." The reader leaves with a mental model, not just information.
5. **Closing that echoes opening.** The best pieces feel circular — the ending resolves the tension created by the opening.
6. **Confidence without arrogance.** "I don't have all the answers" (Ivan) alongside "we think the vast majority" (Cursor). Conviction paired with intellectual honesty.
